SPICALIGN —
Pediatric Hip Spica Cast Positioning Guide

An intraoperative device that helps surgeons position children's hip spica casts for car-seat compatibility — developed with Dr. Michelle Macy at Lurie Children's Hospital through Northwestern's Design Thinking and Communication program.

Mechanical Design Medical Device Prototyping User Research Arduino Servo Control Linear Actuator

1. The Problem

Background

Hip dysplasia in young children (ages 0–5) is often treated with hip spica casts that immobilize both legs at a fixed angle. These casts prevent children from fitting safely into standard car seats — a critical safety requirement before hospital discharge. Specialized spica-compatible car seats can cost several thousand dollars, and many families resort to improvised, unapproved solutions.

Our project partner, Dr. Michelle Macy (MD, MS), a pediatric emergency medicine physician at Lurie Children's Hospital, emphasized the need for a cost-effective intraoperative solution. Currently, surgeons estimate cast positioning by eye — SPICALIGN eliminates that guesswork by providing a physical reference guide during the casting process itself.

2. The Device

How It Works

SPICALIGN attaches to the pole of the spica cast table underneath the patient, staying out of the operating area. While the cast dries, medical personnel operate it via foot pedal commands: a linear actuator raises the device upward, and servo-controlled arms rotate from a stowed position (under the table) to an active position (above the table). The telescoping arms are then manually extended to display the maximum hip width allowed by a standard car seat, giving surgeons a live spatial reference as they position the cast.

ComponentRole
Linear ActuatorTranslates device vertically 12–18 inches via foot pedal; stores unit below table level during surgery
Continuous Rotation Servos (×2)Rotate telescoping arms from stowed (under table) to active (above table) position; selected over DC motors for high torque at low speed and full range of motion
Telescoping ArmsManually extend to display the maximum leg-spread width for a standard car seat; removable for sterilization between surgeries
Foot Pedal ControlsHands-free operation for surgeons working in a sterile field — one pedal raises/lowers, one controls arm rotation
Spica Table MountTriangular bracket attaches to the standard spica cast table pole; future revision to use a bolt-tightened clamp

3. Design Process

Research & Iteration

Our team conducted interviews with orthopedic surgeons, pediatric OR nurses, certified car-seat technicians, and a Ford Design Center workshop specialist to define requirements. Key constraints were compactibility (must not obstruct the operating field), sterility compatibility, and adjustability across patient age groups and car seat categories.

  • Interviewed orthopedic surgeons, pediatric nurses, car-seat technicians, and design specialists to define OR workflow constraints and car seat geometry requirements
  • Evaluated linear actuator vs. custom pulley/crank mechanisms — selected pre-built actuator given timeline and sterilization requirements
  • Evaluated DC motors vs. standard servos vs. continuous rotation servos — selected continuous rotation for full range of motion and high torque at low speed
  • Evaluated PVC pipe, aluminum, and telescoping pointers for arms — selected pointer for budget and weight compliance with servo load limits
  • Built and tested multiple prototype iterations; conducted user testing with clinical observers
  • Documented design rationale, bill of materials, and testing protocols in a formal final design report
